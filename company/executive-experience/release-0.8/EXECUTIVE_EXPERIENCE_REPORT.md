# Executive Experience Report

Purpose: Evaluate whether the live Orendalis staging experience is valuable, comprehensible, and credible to a senior executive.

## Evaluation Persona

**Elena Voss** is a fictional Global VP Sales with 20 years of experience. She leads €150 million in annual revenue across multiple markets and is considering a confidential move into a Chief Revenue Officer role. She expects discretion, concise decision support, strong evidence boundaries, and a product that respects limited executive attention.

No real executive data was used. The existing fictional staging journey supplied the product evidence for this review.

## Overall Finding

The core opportunity-decision journey is the strongest part of Orendalis. It makes evidence, unknowns, confidence, alternatives, and executive ownership explicit. That experience can earn trust.

The surrounding product is not yet equally coherent. The return dashboard does not recognize the completed journey, navigation exposes too many modules at once, Company research does not yet provide company research, and live screens still use internal terms such as “Beta Workflow,” “Release 0.6,” “demonstration,” and “staging Workspace.” The result feels like a credible design-partner product inside an unfinished operating shell.

## What Executives Would Value

- Atlas distinguishes confirmed evidence from unknown information.
- Confidence is described as evidence completeness rather than certainty.
- The executive retains the final decision.
- The workflow advances clearly and preserves completed stages.
- The immutable decision and Career Ledger language create a sense of permanence.
- Workspace-only messaging supports confidentiality.
- Refresh, logout, and return preserve the decision context.

## Material Experience Gaps

### High

1. **The return dashboard is wrong after completion.** It still says “Confirm your professional history” and “Continue Your First Opportunity Assessment” when all eight stages are complete. This immediately reduces confidence in product memory.
2. **Navigation is cognitively expensive.** More than twenty primary destinations are presented with equal visual weight. A time-poor executive must understand the product architecture before understanding what to do.
3. **Company research does not deliver its stated purpose.** The Companies page shows a generic Workspace summary instead of research related to the confirmed company.
4. **The live experience contains internal product language.** “Release 0.6,” “Invitation only,” “Beta Workflow,” “demonstration datasets,” and “staging Workspace” make the executive feel like a tester rather than a client.

### Medium

5. **Atlas is credible but passive.** It identifies eight questions but does not convert them into a focused executive action plan.
6. **Tasks are not actionable.** The product confirms a follow-up exists but does not expose an owner, due date, status, or immediate next action.
7. **The Opportunity page is a summary rather than an intelligence workspace.** It lacks the confirmed facts, unknowns, compensation range, source context, and rationale already present in the workflow.
8. **Career Ledger lacks replay depth.** It confirms preservation but does not let the executive revisit the decision logic in a useful narrative.
9. **Product naming is inconsistent.** The navigation says “AI Assistant,” the page says “Atlas,” and the product elsewhere says “Atlas Executive OS.”
10. **The completed workflow remains visually dense.** A finished executive must scroll through a long historical form-oriented page to revisit the recommendation.

### Low

11. The Beta Workflow page displays the breadcrumb twice.
12. Settings presents product-plan demonstrations and planned entitlements inside the live experience, which distracts from career work.
13. Company Control contains stale readiness statements and is visually overwhelming, although it is founder-only rather than an executive-customer surface.

## Executive Test Questions

- **Would I trust this?** I would trust the decision model more than the surrounding navigation and return experience.
- **Would I understand this?** I would understand the guided journey, but not the importance or order of all primary modules.
- **Would I recommend this?** I would recommend a confidential design-partner trial, not a paid rollout.
- **Would I pay for this?** Not yet.
- **Would this save me time?** The decision workflow could; the rest currently asks me to reconstruct context across thin module summaries.
- **Would I feel smarter?** Atlas’s explicit unknowns and alternatives help. Company and market intelligence do not yet add enough insight.

## Decision

**READY AFTER EXECUTIVE POLISH**

The product is suitable for a tightly supported design-partner engagement after the high-impact experience inconsistencies are corrected. It is not yet a self-explanatory paid executive product.
