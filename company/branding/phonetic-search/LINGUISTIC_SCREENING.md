# Linguistic Screening

## Purpose

This document records a cautious desk screen for pronunciation and unintended meaning across English, Turkish, Bulgarian, German, French, Spanish, Italian, and Dutch.

## Findings

| Candidate | Cross-language observation | Risk |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Tavalen | Latin-alphabet readers can sound it out, but unstressed vowels vary. It resembles Tavalon and appears in unrelated fictional and personal contexts. | Moderate |
| Narelon | No obvious offensive meaning found. English and French readers may vary the first vowel; Turkish, Bulgarian, Spanish, and Italian speakers are more likely to pronounce each vowel fully. | Moderate |
| Ravalen | Generally pronounceable, but it is already a Swedish place name and appears in Spanish and Swedish organization names. | Moderate |
| Darelon | No obvious offensive meaning found. English “dare” influences pronunciation differently from languages that articulate every vowel. | Moderate |
| Renalen | Closely resembles forms of “renal” used internationally in medical contexts. That association is clear, durable, and unsuitable. | High |

## Language-by-language summary

- **English:** all are speakable; vowel reduction creates spelling uncertainty.
- **Turkish:** letters are readable, but the intended English stress and reduced middle vowel would not be automatic.
- **Bulgarian:** transliteration is feasible; exact Latin spelling would still need confirmation.
- **German:** mostly pronounceable; Renalen strongly evokes renal terminology.
- **French:** final consonant treatment and first-vowel interpretation may vary.
- **Spanish and Italian:** full vowel pronunciation improves clarity but differs from likely English delivery.
- **Dutch:** all can be attempted, though English-like and local vowel values may diverge.

No offensive finding was identified in this limited screen. This is not complete linguistic clearance. Native-speaker review in each priority market remains necessary for any future finalist.
