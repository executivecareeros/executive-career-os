# Phonetic Analysis

## Purpose

This document records spoken, hear-and-type, and five-second memory findings for the final research candidates.

## Hear-and-type results

| Candidate | Likely English hearing | Likely alternatives | Ambiguity | Finding |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Tavalen | `TAV-uh-len` | Tavalin, Tavallon, Tavalon | Moderate | Initial sound is clear; the unstressed final vowels are not. |
| Narelon | `NAIR-uh-lon` | Narelan, Nairalon, Narelon | Moderate | Opening vowel and final vowel create repeat-back risk. |
| Ravalen | `RAV-uh-len` | Ravalin, Ravalon, Ravelin | Moderate | Strong opening, but it is easily pulled toward existing words and names. |
| Darelon | `DAIR-uh-lon` | Daralon, Dairalon, Darelan | Moderate | Familiar “dare” opening helps; the full spelling is not self-evident. |
| Renalen | `REN-uh-len` | Renalin, Renallon, Renalen | Moderate | Speakable, but resembles “renal,” creating both spelling and medical associations. |

No finalist achieved **Very Low** or **Low** ambiguity. That alone prevented a recommendation under the founder's stated priority.

## Spoken sentence test

All five can be inserted grammatically into the required sentences, including “Atlas is developed by ___” and “I use ___ to manage my professional life.” Repetition exposed a shared weakness: each sounds like a constructed three-syllable corporate name, and none remains effortless when moved from a prepared sentence to a phone greeting.

Tavalen and Ravalen have the best rhythm. Narelon and Darelon invite a request for spelling. Renalen sounds natural but too close to renal terminology. None passed every sentence without qualification.

## Five-second memory test

- **Tavalen:** rhythmic and reasonably recoverable, but likely to be recalled as Tavalon because that sound pattern is more familiar.
- **Narelon:** the opening is memorable; exact internal vowels are not reliably retained.
- **Ravalen:** strongest recall sound, but geographic and company uses reduce distinctiveness.
- **Darelon:** familiar first syllable aids memory, while the final spelling remains uncertain.
- **Renalen:** memorable through its medical resemblance, which is the wrong memory anchor.

The group demonstrates that pronounceability is not enough: a permanent name also needs exact spelling recall and a clean semantic anchor.
