# Second-Round Analysis

**Board review date:** 12 July 2026  
**Mandate:** Start from zero, seek an iconic parent-company name, and allow only one final recommendation.

## Why iconic names become memorable

The names Apple, Google, Microsoft, Amazon, OpenAI, Stripe, Notion, Canva, Slack, Zoom, Figma, Linear, Datadog, Anthropic, Palantir, Adobe, Oracle, Sony, Nike, Rolex, Tesla, Airbnb, Netflix, Spotify, and SpaceX differ in origin, but they share a psychological architecture.

1. **A clean sound silhouette.** A listener recognizes the whole shape before analyzing its meaning. Strong names have contrast: hard and soft sounds, open and closed vowels, or a decisive stress beat.
2. **Rhythmic economy.** Most resolve in two or three beats. Longer names survive when their internal rhythm is obvious.
3. **An emotional doorway.** They evoke motion, scale, creativity, knowledge, familiarity, or aspiration without explaining a feature.
4. **Confident conversational grammar.** They need no article, apology, or explanation: “I use Stripe”; “Adobe announced”; “the Nike ecosystem.”
5. **Identity before description.** The company teaches the world what the word means. The name is a vessel, not a miniature pitch deck.
6. **Pronunciation consensus.** A first-time reader has one dominant guess. A first-time listener has one dominant spelling.
7. **Cultural portability.** The word is not dependent on a joke, idiom, or narrow English metaphor.
8. **Ownable distinctiveness.** Iconic status needs legal, linguistic, and visual space. Familiarity helps recall; distinctiveness permits ownership.
9. **Meaning accumulated through behavior.** No name becomes legendary by phonetics alone. Product quality, repeated exposure, trust, and cultural moments turn a good word into an icon.

The implication is strict: a new name must be simple enough to enter memory but distinctive enough to become a unique identity.

## Funnel

### 500 → 100

The first cut removed difficult clusters, weak stress, likely spelling errors, category-coded fragments, childish rhythm, and names that did not sound credible in ordinary speech.

### 100 → 50

The second cut removed overly feminine-personal constructions, fantasy-like endings, near-dictionary confusion, and variants too close to established global brands.

### 50 → 20

The third cut prioritized one dominant pronunciation, warmth without softness, credible headline grammar, and 2046 longevity.

### 20 → 5 new challengers

Orenalis, Orenvera, Radelora, Valedara, and Auderina survived the brand screen and returned no `.com` registry record at the moment checked. Emeriva was eliminated after live searches surfaced active UK and Czech companies. Nearly every attractive 5–8 letter alternative returned an existing `.com` registry record.

## Test framework

Every survivor was read aloud in these forms:

- “I use ___.”
- “The founders of ___.”
- “NASDAQ: ___.”
- “I’ve been using ___ for years.”
- “Atlas, open ___.”
- A 12-year-old and a 75-year-old pronunciation simulation.
- “Welcome to the twentieth anniversary of ___.”
- “The CEO of ___”; “___ acquires”; “Powered by ___”; “the ___ ecosystem.”

## Important limitation

A registry 404 means only that the `.com` registry returned no record at the time queried. It does not reserve the domain. Search-engine and public-record knockout checks do not constitute trademark clearance.
