## 1. Orendalis

**Sound:** oh-ren-DAH-liss. Four syllables, but the central **d** gives the word a decisive hinge and a recognizable silhouette.  
**Psychology:** Agency, direction, composure, and institutional depth.  
**Tests:** Natural in coffee, TED, podcast, AI, headline, and anniversary constructions.  
**2046:** “Welcome to the twentieth anniversary of Orendalis.”  
**Weakness:** One syllable beyond the new ideal; requires Orenda-family legal review.  
**Why it leads:** It feels like a company with a history before it has one.

## 2. Orenalis

**Sound:** oh-ren-AH-liss. Smooth and compact at eight letters.  
**Psychology:** Warm and polished, with less authority than Orendalis.  
**Tests:** Easy in conversation and headlines; children and older speakers can pronounce it.  
**2046:** “Welcome to the twentieth anniversary of Orenalis.”  
**Why it lost:** Removing the **d** improves visual economy but weakens recall and structure. It risks being heard as a personal surname or pharmaceutical name. Orendalis has more confidence on stage.

## 3. Orenvera

**Sound:** oh-ren-VEH-ra. Pleasant, open, and globally portable.  
**Psychology:** Warm, truthful, optimistic.  
**Tests:** “Atlas, open Orenvera” works well; “Orenvera acquires…” is credible.  
**2046:** “Welcome to the twentieth anniversary of Orenvera.”  
**Why it lost:** The **vera** ending is crowded across personal names, beauty brands, and “truth” constructions. It is beautiful but less uniquely ownable in memory.

## 4. Radelora

**Sound:** rah-deh-LOR-ah. Musical and visually balanced.  
**Psychology:** Expressive and expansive.  
**Tests:** Natural in most sentences, though first-syllable pronunciation may vary by language.  
**2046:** “Welcome to the twentieth anniversary of Radelora.”  
**Why it lost:** It tilts toward fantasy or luxury cosmetics. It does not project the same trust and disciplined intelligence as Orendalis.

## 5. Valedara

**Sound:** vah-leh-DAH-ra. Warm and ceremonial.  
**Psychology:** Value, valley, and passage.  
**Tests:** Strong anniversary line; acceptable AI command; spelling recall is moderate.  
**2046:** “Welcome to the twentieth anniversary of Valedara.”  
**Why it lost:** It resembles a place name and has greater variation between `Vale-`, `Vali-`, and `Valle-` spellings. It is less immediate after hearing once.

## Challengers that did not make the final five

- **Auderina:** available-domain signal but predominantly a personal name; less neutral and less corporate-ownable.
- **Emeriva:** rejected after active companies surfaced in the UK and Czech Republic.
- **Noveridian:** intelligent but long, five syllables, and more engineered than warm.
- **Talenoria:** pleasing, but its opening pulls the company back toward “talent,” violating industry neutrality.
