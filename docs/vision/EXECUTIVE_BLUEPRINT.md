# Executive Blueprint

> **Purpose:** Define the enduring strategic profile that guides decisions throughout Executive Career OS.

The Blueprint is versioned, evidence-aware, and explicit about confidence and conflicts. Earlier approved revisions remain part of the Career Ledger. Future Atlas output should name the revision and facts it used.

## Overview

The Executive Blueprint is a living articulation of the career an executive intends to build and the conditions under which that career should develop.

It goes beyond a résumé. It captures identity, ambition, constraints, preferences, values, and acceptable trade-offs.

## Career Vision

The desired long-term contribution, scope, impact, and professional legacy.

## Executive DNA

The distinctive combination of strengths, operating patterns, experiences, motivations, and contexts in which the executive creates unusual value.

## Leadership Style

How the executive leads, communicates, makes decisions, builds teams, manages conflict, and operates under uncertainty.

## Executive Principles

The standards and boundaries that should remain durable across employers, roles, and market conditions.

## Compensation Goals

Desired compensation structure, minimum requirements, long-term wealth objectives, and preferences across cash, variable pay, equity, benefits, and risk.

## Industry Preferences

Industries, business models, company stages, ownership structures, and problem domains that are attractive—or should be avoided.

## Geography

Preferred locations, acceptable markets, relocation boundaries, work arrangements, and legal or practical constraints.

## Travel

Acceptable frequency, duration, predictability, and purpose of business travel.

## Family Constraints

Personal commitments and boundaries that materially affect geography, travel, timing, risk, and workload. These should be handled with particular care and privacy.

## Career Objectives

Near-, medium-, and long-term outcomes the executive is deliberately pursuing.

## Board Aspirations

Desired board roles, governance exposure, portfolio composition, readiness gaps, and the experience required to become a credible candidate.

## Negotiation Style

Preferred approach to preparation, anchoring, trade-offs, timing, communication, and relationship preservation.

## Risk Tolerance

The executive’s willingness and capacity to accept uncertainty across company stage, compensation, geography, reputation, role scope, and career continuity.

## How Atlas Uses the Blueprint

Atlas uses the Blueprint to interpret opportunities in context, identify meaningful trade-offs, challenge inconsistent choices, personalize preparation, recall non-negotiables, and explain recommendations.

The Blueprint informs decisions; it does not mechanically determine them. Executives can revise it as their circumstances and ambitions change, while historical versions preserve how their thinking evolved.
