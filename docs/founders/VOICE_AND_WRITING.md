# Voice and Writing

> **Purpose:** Establish writing rules that make every Executive Career OS experience clear, credible, and consistent.

## Core Standard

Write for an intelligent, time-constrained reader making consequential decisions. Lead with the outcome or question. Support it with evidence. State uncertainty plainly.

## Voice

Our voice is calm, precise, respectful, and direct. It is professional without being bureaucratic and human without becoming casual.

## Sentence and Paragraph Length

- Prefer one idea per sentence.
- Use short sentences for decisions, warnings, and next actions.
- Use longer sentences only when the relationship between ideas would otherwise be lost.
- Keep interface paragraphs to two or three sentences.
- Use lists for genuinely parallel evidence, options, or steps.

## Headings

Headings should name the decision, subject, or outcome.

Good: “Compensation conflicts”

Bad: “Things to think about”

Good: “Why Atlas recommends waiting”

Bad: “Atlas insights”

Use sentence case. Avoid clever headings and rhetorical questions unless the user must answer them.

## Recommendations

A recommendation must include the action, evidence, material counterargument, confidence, and missing information.

Good: “Review carefully. The role matches your target scope, but required travel exceeds your confirmed limit.”

Bad: “This exciting role could be a great fit!”

Do not use “should” when the decision belongs to the executive. Prefer “I recommend,” “the evidence supports,” or “consider.”

## Warnings

Name the risk, consequence, and possible response. Do not dramatize.

Good: “The proposed base salary is below your confirmed minimum. Verify the total package before continuing.”

Bad: “Warning! You may be seriously underpaid.”

## Explanations

Separate:

- **Fact:** directly supported by a source;
- **Assessment:** a deterministic interpretation of facts;
- **Assumption:** a condition accepted without confirmation;
- **Recommendation:** a proposed course of action;
- **Confidence:** the quality and completeness of the supporting context.

## Numbers and Dates

Show currency, period, gross/net basis, and whether a value is exact, ranged, or estimated. Use unambiguous dates. Never display missing values as zero.

## Good and Bad Patterns

| Prefer | Avoid |
|---|---|
| “No evidence is available.” | “There doesn’t seem to be anything.” |
| “Confidence is moderate because…” | “Atlas is 63% sure.” |
| “This conflicts with your travel limit.” | “This is not right for you.” |
| “The record was corrected on…” | “The old record was replaced.” |
| “Planned capability” | “Coming soon” without commitment or context |

## Mechanics

- Use active voice when responsibility is known.
- Avoid exclamation marks in routine product communication.
- Avoid slang, idioms, emojis, and inflated adjectives.
- Define an acronym at first use.
- Do not anthropomorphize systems beyond the established Atlas role.
- Never use language designed to create urgency, dependency, or fear of missing out.
